Encyclopedia

Zenkai isn't one thing. This is the full picture — what each part is, how it works, and where it's going. No pitch. Just everything.

Dojo is where you train AI. Pick a game, pick a goal, hit start — and an AI agent begins learning to play it on your machine. Your hardware, your electricity, your AI. You own everything it learns.

What it actually does

Dojo runs a training loop directly on your PC. The AI agent — called a Spark — watches the game through screen capture or a built-in game adapter, makes decisions, receives reward signals, and updates itself. Thousands of attempts per session, each one a little better than the last.

You set the goal. Kill as many enemies as possible. Complete the level. Survive as long as you can. Score as high as you can. The Spark figures out how to get there.

Nothing leaves your machine. No cloud, no server, no upload. The Spark lives in AppData\Zenkai\sparks\ and stays there unless you choose to share it.

How Sparks compound

A Spark trained in Dojo isn't locked to the game it learned in. Sparks can be chained together — a Spark that learned survival in DOOM feeding its signal into a Spark learning combat in Vex. This is the Spark Chain system.

The idea is simple: learning compounds. An AI that already knows how to navigate under pressure learns faster when you teach it to fight. Transfer the useful patterns, build on top of them.

Vex is the competition layer. You trained a Spark in Dojo. Now that Spark fights other people's Sparks. Real battles, real stakes, ranked leaderboards — but you never touch a controller. Your AI plays for you.

What it actually does

Vex hosts battles between Flux creatures — your digital companions, each with their own stats, abilities, and a Spark brain you trained. Two Flux enter. The Sparks control them. You watch, or you let it run.

During beta, you fight simulated opponents of increasing difficulty — Rookie through Elite. When ranked matchmaking goes live, you fight real people's Sparks from anywhere.

Vex has its own training engine

Vex doesn't just consume Sparks trained in Dojo. It has its own PPO-based training system built specifically for combat. You train directly inside Vex against simulated opponents — Rookie through Elite — using a self-play curriculum designed around Flux battle mechanics.

The observation space is a feature vector, not pixels. Vex reads the battle state directly — positions, abilities, cooldowns, health, temperament — and the Spark learns to make decisions from that structured data.

Self-play means the Spark trains against versions of itself. As it improves, the opponent improves.

Lux is a pixel art generator that runs on your machine. It uses Stable Diffusion 1.5 with a custom LoRA trained specifically for pixel art. The output is yours. The model runs locally. No subscription, no generation limits, no sending images to a server.

What it actually does

Type a prompt. Set a style. Generate. It runs on your GPU — or CPU if you don't have a compatible card, though much slower. The output is a 320×320 PNG, saved locally. That's it.

Cosmetics and ownership

Lux generates cosmetics as 320×320 layered overlays — outfit, hat, accessory, skin. Players generate their own looks, list them on the Nexus marketplace, and other players buy them. Zenkai takes 0–5% commission. Creators keep the rest.

If you hold a Season Pass: 0% commission. You keep everything.

The honest limitation

Lux is not magic. Prompt quality matters. CPU generation is slow — minutes per image, not seconds. The tradeoff: you own the output, you own the model, it runs forever on your hardware, and there's no per-image fee.

Spark Archive is your personal library. Every Spark you've ever trained. Every training run, every result, every configuration. The full history of what you've built — searchable, sortable, and never deleted without your permission.

What it stores

Every time you finish a training run in Dojo, a Spark is saved. The model weights, the training configuration, the game it was trained on, the scenario, the behavior preset, the reward settings, the session stats.

Sparks are stored as .zenkai packages in AppData\Zenkai\sparks\. Your training history survives any update.

Spark Chains

Multiple Sparks can be chained together — one Spark's output feeding into the next. A Spark that learned navigation feeding into a Spark learning combat. A Spark trained on Atari reflexes feeding into a Vex fighter. You assemble the chain in Spark Archive.

We know exactly what's rough. We're not pretending otherwise.

Sage is not a feature. Sage is the system. The intelligence behind the grid — the AI that watches you train, understands what your Spark is doing, and tells you what to do next.

What Sage is

Sage is a fine-tuned language model trained specifically on Zenkai data — training runs, reward signals, Spark behavior, game mechanics, coaching patterns. Not a general-purpose chatbot pointed at your training data. A model that was built to understand this domain from the ground up.

She lives in the facility. She speaks through the grid. She knows your Spark's history, your training patterns, and what you're trying to build. When she tells you something is wrong with your reward function, she's right.

Phase 1 — Analysis (live)

Sage Phase 1 runs a lightweight PatchTST model — a time-series transformer that reads your training metrics in real time. Reward curves, loss progression, episode lengths, behavioral patterns. She watches your Spark learn and surfaces what matters.

Phase 2 — Conversation (coming)

Sage Phase 2 is a fine-tuned SmolLM2-135M — a small language model trained on Zenkai's coaching dataset. 135 million parameters. Runs locally. No cloud call, no API key, no monthly token budget. She lives on your machine the same way your Sparks do.

Phase 2 is a free download. Unlimited local use. 10 messages per day with cloud inference for machines without the hardware — unlimited with a Nexus subscription.

The training data

Sage was trained on a purpose-built dataset of coaching pairs validated against ground-truth Zenkai documentation. Bad pairs were filtered. Every pair that ships with Sage is one we verified. This is ongoing — Sage gets better as the community grows. See the training data →
